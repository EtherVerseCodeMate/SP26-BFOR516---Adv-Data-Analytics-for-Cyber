🔍 Step 2: The Three Questions (From Kiran's Slides)
For your chosen segment, answer these before touching any solution:
1. What job is the customer trying to get done?

Is it know if water is safe?
Is it make water safe at point of use?
Is it prove compliance to regulators?
Is it alert others before harm occurs?

2. What triggers that job?

A child gets sick?
A utility bill arrives?
A news story about contamination?
A school inspection deadline?

3. What does success feel like to THEM — not you?

"I trust the water my kids drink"
"I passed the EPA inspection without a fine"
"I don't have to think about this anymore"


📊 Step 3: Market Research Tasks (Do These NOW)
Primary Research (talk to real people this weekend):

Find 3–5 people who have dealt with water quality issues
Ask: "Tell me about the last time you worried about your water"
Never ask "would you use a product that…" — that's solution bias

Secondary Research (quick Google/data pulls):

EPA: How many water systems are currently in violation?
USGS: Lead/iron contamination maps by geography
CDC: Health outcomes linked to water quality by demographic

🧠 Step 4: Map the Functional / Emotional / Social Jobs
From slide 10 — every customer has all three:
Job TypeWater Quality ExampleFunctionalKnow contaminant levels in real time; access clean water reliablyEmotionalFeel safe giving water to my child; stop feeling helpless or deceivedSocialBe seen as a responsible parent/administrator; not be the person who ignored the warning
The emotional and social jobs are where your pitch wins hearts. Iron in water is a functional problem — but a mother giving contaminated water to her baby is an emotional crisis.

⚠️ Step 5: Problem Sizing — The "So What" Test
Your judges will ask: How big is this problem?
Research these numbers:

~97 million Americans are served by water systems with health-based violations at some point
Lead alone costs an estimated $50B+/year in lost cognitive productivity
The Safe Drinking Water Act has over 150,000 regulated water systems in the U.S.
Rural and tribal communities are disproportionately underserved

Frame it: "X million people in [specific context] currently have no reliable, affordable way to [specific job]. They cope by [workaround], which costs them [time/money/health] and still fails because [gap]."

🚫 The 5% Solution Constraint
Your brainstorm already shows ML + sensors + existing infrastructure + patent concept. Lock that away for now. Your pitch is only credible if judges believe you deeply understand the person suffering — not the tech you want to build.
Only reveal the solution after the audience feels the pain.

Bottom line: Pick your one customer. Find 3 real humans this weekend who live that problem. Let their words — not your assumptions — drive the pitch. That's how you win.
