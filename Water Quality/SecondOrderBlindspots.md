# Second-Order Blindspots — What's Still Not Questioned

*March 2026 | BFOR 516 Capstone — Water Quality Pitch Prep*
*Status: Problem validation phase — primary research NOT yet completed*

---

## The Problem Frame Itself May Be Wrong

Every document so far assumes the problem is **information access** — people don't know if their water is safe. But there are four competing hypotheses, only one of which can be primary:

| Hypothesis | Problem Statement | Implies Different Solution |
|---|---|---|
| **Information Gap** (current assumption) | People don't know their water is unsafe | Real-time monitoring, data layer |
| **Trust Gap** | People *suspect* it's unsafe but don't trust any source enough to act | Credibility and accountability system |
| **Agency Gap** | People *know* it's unsafe but can't do anything about it | Policy/legal tools, not detection |
| **Attention Gap** | People know, distrust, and have no bandwidth to care amid bigger survival pressures | Radically simplified intervention |

**Which one is actually true for your beachhead customer?** Unknown. The answer changes everything — including whether technology is even the right lever.

A mom in Newark who lived through the 2019 crisis doesn't have an information problem. She has a trust and agency problem. Giving her a better dashboard doesn't fix that.

**This is the primary question the weekend interviews must answer.**

---

## The "Change The World" Scope Is Still Too Small

The actual world-changing question buried in the research:

> **What if the reason water contamination persists for decades is that the data infrastructure to prove harm at the individual level has never existed — so accountability is impossible?**

Flint lasted years partly because residents couldn't produce irrefutable, granular, real-time evidence that held specific officials and utilities legally accountable. The "change the world" frame isn't *better filters* — it's **making water contamination legally and politically impossible to ignore or deny.**

That's a fundamentally different mission than a safety data layer.

---

## Blindspot: Climate Is About to Make This Exponentially Worse

None of the prior docs have touched this. Water quality is not a static infrastructure problem — it's an accelerating one:

- Extreme heat → algal blooms in reservoirs, spiking cyanotoxin levels in municipal systems
- Flooding → overwhelms treatment plants, drives sewage contamination events
- Drought → concentrates contaminants in shrinking groundwater
- Wildfire ash (PFAS, heavy metals) → enters watersheds and distribution systems for months post-fire

The EPA projects climate will create **episodic, unpredictable contamination events** in systems that currently test clean. This is not a future scenario — it's happening in LA, Phoenix, and Asheville after Helene (2024).

**Reframe:** This isn't a legacy infrastructure problem. It's a **climate resilience problem** with a legacy infrastructure starting point. That triples the addressable population and makes the mission genuinely urgent at a civilizational scale.

The competition opened with three challenge topics: Aging, Extreme Weather Disasters, and Water Quality. Water quality intersects all three. That is not a coincidence.

---

## Blindspot: Who Actually Has the Power to Change This?

The JTBD framework has been entirely focused on the *sufferer*. In a systems problem, the person with the highest pain is rarely the person with the power to change the system.

| Actor | Their Job | Their Fear | Their Budget |
|---|---|---|---|
| Renter / parent | Keep family safe | Child harmed by invisible threat | Near zero |
| Landlord | Avoid liability | Lawsuit, code violation | Moderate — if legally exposed |
| School district | Protect students, avoid liability | Parent lawsuit, news story, federal violation | Real — federal mandate exists |
| City utility | Meet EPA compliance metrics | Being the next Flint on the news | Large — federal infrastructure funds available |
| State regulator | Enforce SDWA | Federal enforcement action | Limited but real |
| Federal EPA | Set and enforce standards | Congressional oversight, NGO litigation | Significant |
| Trial lawyers / EJ NGOs | Enforce accountability | Losing cases due to lack of evidence | Contingency-funded — significant |

**Most underserved actor in the research so far: trial lawyers and environmental justice NGOs.** They have budget, motivation, and direct incentive to fund tools that generate irrefutable evidence of harm. This is a completely different beachhead with real willingness to pay — and it serves the renter indirectly but powerfully.

School districts are a close second: federal mandate (EPA Lead and Copper Rule), budget, install rights, and direct accountability to parents.

---

## The Uncomfortable Question: Is the Gap Technical or Organizational?

All research so far assumes the solution involves technology. But the most successful water quality intervention in recent U.S. history — the Flint recovery — was driven by **a pediatrician with a spreadsheet and a journalist with a FOIA request**, not a tech platform.

> Is the actual gap technical, or is it *organizational and political*?

If the real problem is that affected communities lack the capacity to generate and weaponize their own data, the solution might be a **community data collection protocol + legal partnerships** — not sensors or ML.

That's not less interesting. It may be more fundable, more immediately deployable, and more genuinely world-changing than another IoT device.

**This is testable in interviews.** Ask: "When you found out there was a problem, what stopped you from doing something about it?" The answer reveals whether the blocker is information, trust, money, time, or power.

---

## Revised Interview Plan — What Each Conversation Must Determine

You have zero primary research. Before committing to any problem frame, you need at least one person in each of these buckets:

### Interview Target 1: A Renter or Parent in an Affected City
- Someone who has experienced a water quality event (boil notice, discolored water, Flint-adjacent city)
- Key question: "What did you *actually do* after you found out?" (not what they wish existed)
- Diagnostic: Did they do nothing? Buy bottled water? Call the city? Move? The action reveals the real gap.

### Interview Target 2: A Utility or Public Health Worker
- Someone who works at a city water authority, state health department, or EPA region
- Key question: "Walk me through what happens inside your organization when a contamination event is detected."
- Diagnostic: Where does the information die before it reaches the resident?

### Interview Target 3: A School Facilities Manager or Nurse (pre-1986 building)
- Key question: "Tell me about when you got your water fountain test results. What happened next?"
- Diagnostic: Did they have to notify parents? Were they given clear guidance? Do they still worry?

**Bonus target if available:** An environmental justice attorney or NRDC/EWG field staffer.
- Key question: "What evidence do you wish you had in a case involving water contamination?"

## The Four Hypotheses — How to Test Each in Conversation

| Hypothesis | What It Looks Like in an Interview |
|---|---|
| **Information Gap** | Person says: "I had no idea. I would have acted if I'd known." They had no access to the data. |
| **Trust Gap** | Person says: "I knew something was wrong, but every source has an agenda." They had the information but couldn't verify it. |
| **Agency Gap** | Person says: "I knew it was bad. I called the city, my landlord, everyone. Nothing changed." They had information and trust but no leverage. |
| **Attention Gap** | Person says: "I thought about it once after a news story and then had to get back to work, the kids, everything." The problem doesn't compete for priority in their life. |

One or more of these will emerge clearly in 2–3 conversations. **Lock the problem frame after the interviews, not before.**

---

## Bottom Line

The research corpus is exceptional preparation *for conversations* — not a substitute for them. What's needed before locking anything:

1. **Which hypothesis is primary** — information, trust, agency, or attention?
2. **Who has power AND incentive** to change this (likely not the renter)?
3. **Is the gap technical or organizational** — does the solution require sensors, or spreadsheets and subpoenas?

Answering these three questions across 2–3 weekend interviews will produce a problem statement that no judge can knock down — because it will be grounded in what real people actually said and actually did.
