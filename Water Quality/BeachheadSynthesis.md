# Synthesis: Rural Wells vs. Legacy Municipal Systems
### Resolving the Beachhead Tension

*March 2026 | BFOR 516 Capstone — Water Quality Pitch Prep*

---

## The Core Disagreement

| Dimension | Rural Well | Legacy Municipal |
|---|---|---|
| Scale | ~43M (unregulated) | ~21–23M (active violations) |
| Urgency | Slow burn, invisible | Crisis-triggered, acute |
| Emotional charge | Resigned anxiety | Betrayal + moral injury |
| Customer agency | Moderate (can test independently) | Near-zero (renter, can't fix pipes) |
| Market size signal | Diffuse | Concentrated in known cities |
| Pitch drama | Lower | Much higher |

---

## The Synthesis Verdict: Legacy Municipal Wins the Pitch, Rural Wins the Product

**For the pitch competition → Legacy municipal.** The emotional core is sharper. "I pay for this water every month and my city lied to me" lands harder than "I'm on a well and don't test it enough." Judges feel the Flint resonance instantly. The $20B bottled water market exists *because* of this betrayal.

**For long-term product-market fit → the populations overlap.** Both segments share the *exact same core job:*

> **"Know whether the water I'm giving my family is safe TODAY — in plain language, without needing a scientist, a lawyer, or a landlord's permission."**

That's your North Star job statement. It works for both audiences.

---

## The Unified Problem Statement

> **Millions of American families — in crumbling cities and forgotten rural counties alike — have no affordable, real-time way to know if their tap water is safe today. They pay hundreds of dollars a year on bottled water and unverified filters that may not even address their specific contaminants, while utilities publish annual reports written to minimize alarm, not inform families. The people most exposed are renters, parents of young children, and low-income communities of color who have zero ability to fix the pipes and zero trusted source telling them the truth.**

---

## Three Numbers That Win the Room

**1.** "9.2 million lead service lines still in the ground — EPA gives utilities **10 years** to replace them. Those families are exposed *now*."

**2.** "The average affected household spends **$500–700/year** on bottled water — a workaround that doesn't protect them during bathing, cooking, or making baby formula."

**3.** "Most people using pitcher filters don't know that standard Brita **does not remove lead** unless it carries NSF/ANSI 53 certification. They think they're protected. They're not."

Stat #3 is the gut-punch. The existing workaround is actively creating a false sense of safety.

## JTBD Arc (Bridges to the 5% Solution Without Revealing It)

| Job Layer | Statement |
|---|---|
| Functional | Know contaminant levels at the point of use, in real time, without a $300 lab test |
| Emotional | Stop the quiet guilt every time you pour a glass for your kid |
| Social | Have evidence — not just anxiety — when your landlord, utility, or pediatrician dismisses your concern |

**Solution tease (no architecture revealed):**
> *"We're building a way for families to know — in plain language — whether their water is safe today, using infrastructure that already exists in their home."*

That framing works for a municipal apartment and a rural farmhouse. The pipe is already there. The need is already there. The $20B market proves people are already paying to solve it badly.

## Primary Research — Who to Find and What to Ask

**Target (fastest to find this weekend):**
- Any parent in pre-1986 housing who has ever bought bottled water "just to be safe"
- Any teacher/school staff who has dealt with water fountain testing notices

**Single opening question:**
> *"Tell me about the last time you thought about whether your tap water was safe. What happened?"*

Their words become your opening line. Don't ask anything else until they stop talking.

**Where to find them fast:**
- Facebook: "Flint Water Crisis Survivors," Newark NJ tenant groups, Chicago renters groups
- Reddit: r/Flint, r/chicago, r/newjersey, r/homeowners
- In person: school dropoff, community center, laundromat in an older neighborhood
