## What This Data Means — Plain Language

### The Disinfection Byproduct Story

TTHMs (Total Trihalomethanes) and HAAs (Haloacetic Acids) are **disinfection byproducts** — chemicals created when chlorine reacts with organic matter in the Hudson River source water. The city is doing the right thing by chlorinating to kill pathogens. That process is producing carcinogens as a side effect.

- **TTHMs at 68.2 ppb** — below the legal limit of 80 ppb, so the city passes compliance. But 455 times over the health guideline.
- **HAA5 at 34.1 ppb** — below legal limit of 60 ppb. Passes. 341 times over the health guideline.
- **Neither has a real-time alert system.** The annual Consumer Confidence Report will report an annual average below the legal limit and check a box.

This is the regulatory information betrayal in a single table. The government says "passes." EWG says "up to 560 times over the health threshold." The resident receives neither number in plain language at the tap.

### The Chromium-6 (Hexavalent) Finding

- Detected at **0.118 ppb** — 5.9 times the EWG health guideline
- No legal limit exists for hexavalent chromium in federal drinking water law
- This is the "Erin Brockovich" chemical — the contaminant at the center of the most famous water contamination lawsuit in U.S. history
- Troy residents are drinking it legally

### The Radium Finding

- **Radioactive** — combined radium at 11 times the health guideline
- Below the legal limit, so it passes compliance
- No letter was mailed. No alert was issued.

## The Filter Reality for Troy Residents

EWG's own filter recommendation for Troy water:

| Filter Type | Contaminants Above Guidelines Removed | Cost |
|---|---|---|
| Carbon filter (standard Brita) | 8 of 10 | Low |
| Reverse osmosis | 10 of 10 + 7 others | High |
| Ion exchange | Softens water, reduces some | Moderate |

**The Brita gap, Troy-specific:** A standard carbon pitcher filter removes 8 of Troy's 10 above-guideline contaminants. It does NOT remove radium or chromium (hexavalent). Most residents don't know this. And even the 8 it addresses assumes the filter is current — not fouled by iron or manganese (also detected in Troy's water).

**Reverse osmosis removes everything** — but costs $200–$500 installed, requires a plumber, wastes water, and a renter cannot install it.

## EWG Tools — Competitive Landscape and Market Signal

EWG operates three relevant tools (ewg.org/apps):

| Tool | What It Does | What It Proves | What It Fails |
|---|---|---|---|
| **EWG Tap Water Database** | System-level contaminant data with health context, 2013–2024 averages | The market wants plain-language health safety ratings — EWG proved this for water | Not real-time; not address-specific; not shareable as a live status; not connected to service line inventory |
| **EWG Healthy Living App** | Scan a consumer product → instant safety rating | People will pull out their phones to check if something is safe. Same behavior applies to water. | Doesn't exist for water at the tap level |
| **EWG Skin Deep** | Ingredient-level safety ratings for personal care products | EWG already built the UX model: take complex data, produce a plain-language safety verdict | Not water |

**The strategic insight from EWG's app suite:** EWG proved that consumers will use their phones to verify safety in real time — for shampoo, for food, for sunscreen. The same behavior, the same UX pattern, has never been applied to the thing they drink every day from a faucet. EWG built the reference model. Nobody has applied it to tap water at the address level.

**Why EWG itself isn't the solution:** The EWG Tap Water Database shows Troy has 10 contaminants above health guidelines. That data is 1–3 years old. It's system-wide, not building-specific. There's no "is this safe right now?" verdict. There's no QR code on the tap. There's no alert when something changes. And critically — it requires the resident to know to go look, rather than the answer coming to them.

**The pitch line for EWG:**
> "EWG proved people will use their phones to check if something is safe. They built it for shampoo. They built it for food. The Tap Water Database is the closest they got to water — and it still can't answer whether your tap is safe today at your address. That's the gap."
