## THE SCRIPT

---

### MOVE 1 — Open With a Story (0:00–0:60)
*Rubric: Problem Definition & Urgency — opens with human reality, not statistics*

**Say this:**

> "Ten minutes from this building, a city has lead levels in its drinking water higher than Flint, Michigan at the height of its crisis.
>
> 35.4 parts per billion. The EPA says act at 15. Troy has been above that for over a decade.
>
> Last year, the city mailed letters to tens of thousands of residents. The letters said: *'Unknown Service Line.'* Not a clean bill of health. Not an explanation. Just: *Unknown.* We don't know if your pipes are safe. We're working on it.
>
> One of those residents is a mother. She has a two-year-old. She opens that letter, and she does what every parent does — she Googles. She finds nothing actionable. So she drives to the store, buys bottled water, hauls it up three flights of stairs, and goes to sleep not knowing if she already gave her child too much of what she was trying to avoid.
>
> That is where our problem starts."

*Pause. Let it land.*

---

### MOVE 2 — State the Problem (1:00–1:30)
*Rubric: Problem Definition & Urgency — one sentence, beachhead persona, moral injury framing*

**Say this:**

> "22 million Americans are served by water systems that violate federal health standards. 9.2 million lead service lines are still in the ground. And the average household caught in this crisis spends $500 to $700 every year on bottled water — a workaround that doesn't protect them while bathing, cooking, or making baby formula.
>
> But here's what breaks it: most people using pitcher filters don't know that a standard Brita does not remove lead. 39% of certified filters fail under real-world conditions. The backup plan is failing quietly, and nobody is correcting it.
>
> The person who suffers most is a low-income renter in a pre-1986 building with children under 12, who has no ability to replace her pipes, no money to test her water, and no trusted source telling her whether the water is safe *today.*"

---

### MOVE 3 — Why Existing Solutions Fail (1:30–2:00)
*Rubric: Innovation — name and dismiss competitors in one breath*

**Say this:**

> "The solutions people reach for all fail the same job.
>
> Flo by Moen detects leaks and burst pipes. It protects your house. It doesn't tell you if your water is safe to drink.
>
> Tap Score sends you a lab report in five days. A snapshot. One inbox. No continuous signal.
>
> And the city's annual Consumer Confidence Report? Written by lawyers. Published months after collection. Uses system-wide averages that legally comply while your specific building is spiking.
>
> Every existing solution requires money you don't have, technical knowledge you don't have, or patience for a process designed for utilities — not families."

---

### MOVE 4 — The Innovation (2:00–2:30)
*Rubric: Innovation — the conceptual gap, not the architecture*

**Say this:**

> "What doesn't exist — what has never existed — is a continuous, shared, plain-language safety signal at the tap. One answer that the renter, the landlord, the school principal, the utility, and the regulator can all see at the same time.
>
> Not a lab report. Not a compliance PDF. A status. Safe or not safe, today, for your specific address.
>
> We're building that data layer — using infrastructure that already exists. The pipes are there. The city lab data is already being collected. The sensors are available off the shelf. Nobody has assembled it into a signal that actually reaches the family."

---

### MOVE 5 — Show the Prototype (2:30–3:30)
*Rubric: Prototype — demo live, physical prop, QR code flow*

**Say this (while showing):**

> "This is what that looks like."

*[Hold up phone or scan QR code live. Show the prototype.]*

> "A resident scans a QR code — mounted on their tap, handed out by their building manager, or texted by their utility. No app download. No account creation. They see this."

*[Show the status screen: address, contaminant levels in plain language, recommended action, last test time]*

> "Iron: elevated — discoloring but not a health risk today. Lead: below action level — safe for drinking. Last updated: 4 hours ago.
>
> That's it. That's what 22 million people don't have right now."

*[Show building manager view if built]*

> "And the building manager sees this: three units flagged for follow-up. One fountain at the school pending re-test. This is the same data — two different jobs."

---

### MOVE 6 — Go-to-Market (3:30–4:00)
*Rubric: Feasibility — B2B2C model, specific city, federal funding anchor*

**Say this:**

> "We're not selling to the renter. We can't — she can't install anything. We sell to whoever has install rights and liability: the building owner, the school district, or the city itself.
>
> Year one: one city. Troy, New York. Already has a federal grant — $16.7 million to track and replace lead lines. Already using AI to identify pipes. Already sending letters to residents. What it doesn't have is the resident-facing side of that system.
>
> School districts are already federally mandated to test their fountains under the America's Water Infrastructure Act. They're already buying testing. We're a better version of what they're already required to purchase.
>
> The Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act allocated $15 billion specifically for lead pipe replacement and water quality monitoring. The buyer has a budget line. The mandate exists. The data exists. We translate it."

---

### MOVE 7 — Impact and the World-Change Argument (4:00–4:45)
*Rubric: Impact — individual → systemic → civic accountability*

**Say this:**

> "Here's the 'change the world' argument.
>
> Lead exposure costs the United States $50.9 billion every year — in lost cognitive productivity, special education costs, lifetime reduced earnings. More than asthma, autism, and childhood cancer combined. That number is not a natural disaster. It's the bill for a solvable problem that no one has been politically unable to ignore long enough to fix.
>
> And climate is making it worse. PFAS from wildfire ash. Flooding overwhelming treatment plants. Drought concentrating contaminants. The systems that test clean today will fail tomorrow. The exposure window is growing.
>
> But here's what changes the world: for the first time, individual exposure data and regulatory accountability data become the same dataset. When 21 million people can point to the same independent safety signal about their water, you cannot bury a PDF. When every parent's phone says 'unsafe today' in the same neighborhood, the politics around slow fixes changes. Contamination becomes politically indefensible — not just scientifically documented.
>
> We're not building a filter. We're building the accountability infrastructure that makes the crisis impossible to ignore."

---

### MOVE 8 — Close (4:45–5:00)
*Rubric: Pitch Quality — end on mission, not product*

**Say this:**

> "A mother in Troy opened a letter last year that said 'Unknown.' She still doesn't know.
>
> She should know. And after tonight, we're going to build the thing that tells her."

*Stop. Do not add anything.*

## Q&A PREPARATION

### "Tap Score already does this."
> "Tap Score is a snapshot — you mail a sample, wait five days, get one PDF in one inbox. We're answering what's in your water right now, continuously, shareable with your landlord and your school. Completely different job."

### "Your renter can't install anything."
> "Correct. That's why we don't sell to the renter. We sell to the building owner, school district, or city program. The renter scans a QR code and reads a status. No install. No account."

### "What happens in 2035 when lead pipes are replaced?"
> "Lead is the wedge, not the product. PFAS has no replacement timeline — the EPA set the MCL last year and there is no infrastructure fix on the horizon. Climate events are creating new contamination in systems that test clean. The safety telemetry layer is permanent infrastructure. Lead just makes the need obvious today."

### "Why hasn't the city built this?"
> "Cities collect the data — but their systems are built for compliance reporting, not family-level communication. The CCR is written by lawyers to satisfy regulators, not to inform parents. Nobody is rewarded for making this legible to the resident. We're the translation layer between data that already exists and the people who need it."

### "What's your business model?"
> "SaaS to utilities and building managers. The buyer has a compliance mandate and a federal budget line. The resident accesses for free. We're not monetizing the family — we're solving the city's communication and liability problem."

## THREE NUMBERS TO MEMORIZE

**35.4 ppb** — Troy's 90th percentile lead level; Flint at crisis was lower.

**9.2 million** — lead service lines still in the ground nationally; EPA gives utilities 10 years.

**39%** — certified filters that fail under real-world particulate lead conditions.

Each number escalates the betrayal: infrastructure fails → workarounds fail → the "safe" backup fails.

---

## WHAT NOT TO SAY

- "Utilize" / "leverage" / "synergy" / "paradigm shift" — kills authenticity
- "There's nothing like this" before naming what exists and why it fails
- "Global water crisis" — too broad, zero urgency
- "Our ML model is the innovation" — judges hear this from every team
- Any financial projections or hockey-stick slides — one sentence on revenue model max
- Reading from slides after the story — memorize Moves 1–3 cold
