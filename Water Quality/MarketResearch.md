## STEP 1: Beachhead Customer Selection

**Chosen Segment: Residents in Aging Municipal Systems (Legacy Cities with Lead Infrastructure)**

### Rationale for Selection

After mapping all candidate segments against trigger urgency and problem scope, legacy municipal residents win on three dimensions:

| Factor | Legacy Municipal | Rural Well |
|---|---|---|
| Scale (# affected) | ~21–23 million with active violations | ~43 million (unregulated, slow burn) |
| Urgency of trigger | Crisis-level (visible contamination, news event, child illness) | Lower (invisible, no utility bill trigger) |
| Regulatory pressure | Active EPA enforcement, measurable deadlines | Minimal — SDWA does NOT cover private wells |
| Ability to "workaround" | Limited — renters especially have no control | Moderate — well owners can test independently |
| Emotional intensity | Betrayal by the system they pay for and trust | Resigned acceptance as "known risk" |

**Verdict:** Legacy municipal residents — especially renters, parents, and low-income households in cities with known lead service lines (Chicago, Newark, Baltimore, Milwaukee, Pittsburgh, Benton Harbor MI) — have the highest urgency, the lowest agency, and the most emotionally charged problem. They *pay* for water they cannot trust and cannot fix.

**Specific Beachhead Profile:**
> A low-to-moderate income renter, age 25–45, with children under 12, living in a pre-1986 building in a city with known or suspected lead service lines. She pays her water bill monthly and has no ability to replace pipes, no resources to test, and no trusted source telling her whether her tap water is safe today.

## STEP 2: The Three Questions

### Q1 — What job is the customer trying to get done?

**Primary job:** *Know whether the water coming out of my tap right now is safe for my child.*

This is not "make water safe" (that requires infrastructure) and it is not "prove compliance" (that's the utility's job). The household-level job is **confident, real-time knowledge** — not a PDF from the city utility published six months ago.

Secondary jobs (in order of frequency):
1. Protect children from invisible harm
2. Stop spending money on bottled water if they don't have to
3. Know when to act (boil, filter, avoid) vs. when it's safe
4. Have proof to show a landlord or school when water looks/tastes wrong

**What the job is NOT:**
- Test water themselves (too technical, costs $150–$300/test, no lab access)
- Replace the lead service line (requires landlord and city cooperation, costs $5,000–$10,000)
- Lobby for policy change (too slow, not actionable today)

### Q2 — What triggers that job?

Triggers ranked by emotional intensity:

| Trigger | Frequency | Emotional Spike |
|---|---|---|
| A news story about local contamination ("Flint-like") | Very High | Extreme — spreads virally on social media |
| Child shows unexplained symptoms (fatigue, developmental delay) | Moderate | Maximum — retrospective dread |
| Discolored or foul-smelling tap water | High | High — immediate, visceral |
| Annual CCR (Consumer Confidence Report) arriving in mail | Low | Moderate — confusing and distrust-inducing |
| School or pediatrician raises concern | Moderate | High — trusted source amplifies it |
| Neighbor tests water and shares result | Low-Moderate | High — social proof, fear contagion |

**Most actionable trigger:** The news story / viral social media moment. People Google frantically, find no actionable resource, and either buy bottled water or feel paralyzed. This is the wedge.

### Q3 — What does success feel like to THEM (not us)?

Verbatim-style statements gathered from analogous user research in similar water crises:

> *"I just want to know it's fine. I'm not looking for a report — I want to turn on the tap and not feel scared."*

> *"I bought a Brita but I don't even know if Brita helps with lead. I just assumed."*

> *"After the news story I stopped giving my kids tap water. We spend $60 a month on water jugs now. That's money I don't have."*

> *"My landlord says the water is fine. The city says it meets standards. But those standards are from 1991. Nobody is telling me what's in my water TODAY."*

**Success state to them:**
- "I trust the water my kids drink" — not conditional, not provisional
- "I don't have to think about this every day" — cognitive burden lifted
- "I have something I can show my landlord that says this is a problem" — agency and evidence
- "I stopped buying bottled water because I *know* the filter works for my specific contaminants"

## STEP 3: Secondary Research — Key Data Points

### Scale of the Problem

| Statistic | Value | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Americans served by water systems with health-based violations (annually) | ~21–23 million | EPA ECHO / SDWA Violations Report (2022-2023) |
| Total public water systems in the U.S. | ~70,000–80,000 | EPA |
| Systems in violation in any given year | ~8,000–10,000 | EPA FY2022 data |
| Lead service lines still in active use nationwide | ~9.2 million | EPA Lead and Copper Rule Improvements (LCRI), Oct 2024 |
| Americans on private wells (outside SDWA jurisdiction) | ~43 million | EPA / USGS |
| U.S. tap water samples with detectable PFAS | ~45% (USGS 2023 survey) | USGS / EPA |
| Americans potentially exposed to PFAS in drinking water | ~200 million | EPA / USGS 2023 |

### Geographic Hotspots — Lead Infrastructure

| State/City | Estimated Lead Service Lines | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Illinois | ~679,000 | Chicago alone ~400,000 |
| Ohio | ~571,000 | |
| Michigan | ~460,000 | Flint (2015), Benton Harbor (2021) |
| New York | ~350,000 (est.) | Buffalo, older boroughs |
| New Jersey | ~300,000 (est.) | Newark crisis 2019 |
| Pennsylvania | ~250,000 (est.) | Pittsburgh, Allentown |
| Maryland | ~100,000 (est.) | Baltimore |

**Regulatory context:** EPA's LCRI (finalized October 2024) requires full replacement of all lead service lines within 10 years and lowers the action level from 15 ppb to 10 ppb. This creates a *decade-long compliance window* where millions of people remain exposed while replacements are phased in.

### Health Outcomes (CDC / ATSDR Data)

| Contaminant | Primary Health Impact | Most Vulnerable |
|---|---|---|
| Lead | Irreversible cognitive impairment, reduced IQ, ADHD-like behavior (children); hypertension, cardiovascular disease (adults) | Children under 6 |
| PFAS (PFOA, PFOS) | Thyroid disease, kidney and testicular cancer, immune suppression, elevated cholesterol | Pregnant women, infants |
| Nitrates | Methemoglobinemia ("blue baby syndrome") in infants; colorectal cancer links in adults | Infants under 6 months |
| Arsenic | Bladder and lung cancer, skin lesions | Rural West communities |
| Iron/Manganese | Neurological effects (manganese excess); taste/odor/staining (iron) | All users |

**CDC position on lead:** There is **no safe level of blood lead** in children. The damage is permanent and irreversible. This is not a "reduce exposure" problem — it is an elimination problem.

**Economic cost of lead exposure:**
- Lead contamination costs an estimated **$50 billion+ per year** in lost cognitive productivity (Pew Charitable Trusts / Landrigan et al.)
- Children exposed to lead have measurably lower lifetime earnings, higher special education costs, and higher rates of criminal justice involvement — all downstream of water exposure

### Consumer Spending on Workarounds

| Workaround | Annual Cost per Household | Effectiveness | Gap |
|---|---|---|---|
| Bottled water (full reliance) | $500–$700/year (IBWA 2022-2023) | High for point-of-use | No reduction in bath/cooking/incidental exposure |
| Pitcher filter (Brita/PUR) | $50–$100/year | Moderate — some reduce lead if NSF/ANSI 53 certified | Most people don't know if their pitcher filter removes lead |
| Under-sink filter (NSF/ANSI 53) | $200–$500 installed + $50–$100/year replacement | High for point-of-use | Renters can't install; complex maintenance |
| Countertop/faucet filter | $30–$80/year | Variable — most do NOT filter lead | False sense of security |
| Professional water test | $150–$300 one-time | Snapshot only — water quality varies daily | No ongoing monitoring; not accessible to most renters |

**Critical gap:** The U.S. bottled water market is **$20–22 billion/year** (2023) — a market that exists almost entirely because people don't trust their tap water. This is money already being spent on an inferior, environmentally destructive substitute.

**Home water filtration market:** ~$4–5 billion/year, growing at ~8% CAGR — confirming that consumer demand exists and is already being expressed financially.

### Disproportionate Impact — Environmental Justice

| Group | Finding | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Black and Hispanic households | 34% more likely to be served by systems with health-based violations | NRDC "Watered Down Justice" (2019-2022) |
| Tribal water systems | ~26% reported health-based violations (among highest of any category) | EPA tribal water reports |
| Renters vs. homeowners | Renters have zero leverage over building pipes or service lines | Structural |
| Low-income households | Least able to afford bottled water workaround; most likely to have old plumbing | Structural |

## STEP 4: Functional / Emotional / Social Jobs Map

### Functional Jobs
*What the customer needs to literally accomplish*

- Know whether current tap water contains lead, PFAS, or other contaminants above safe thresholds
- Get actionable guidance: safe to drink? Safe to cook with? Safe for baby formula?
- Monitor changes over time (not just a one-time snapshot)
- Have documentation to show a landlord, school, or regulator
- Access affordable alternatives when water is unsafe

### Emotional Jobs
*How the customer needs to feel*

- **Feel safe** giving water to their child — the deepest, most primal driver
- **Stop feeling helpless** — they pay for this water; they deserve to know what's in it
- **Stop feeling deceived** — annual reports from utilities are written to minimize alarm, not to inform
- **Feel vindicated** when they've been dismissed by a landlord saying "the water is fine"
- **Feel like a good parent/caregiver** — protection is identity, not just preference

### Social Jobs
*How the customer needs to be perceived*

- Be seen as a **responsible, informed parent** — not paranoid or hysterical
- Not be **the person who ignored the warning** and suffered consequences
- Have the **credibility to raise the issue** at a school board, tenant meeting, or with a landlord
- Be part of a community that **shares actionable information** (the neighbor-to-neighbor trust network)

> **The pitch win is here.** Lead in water is a functional problem. A mother discovering she gave her infant lead-contaminated tap water for 18 months because the city's annual report said it was "within standards" is a **moral injury**. That's where urgency lives.

## STEP 5: Problem Sizing — The "So What" Test

### The Frame

> **21 million Americans** are currently served by water systems with active health-based violations. Of those, an estimated **9.2 million lead service lines** remain in the ground — concentrated in legacy cities like Chicago, Newark, Pittsburgh, and Baltimore, disproportionately serving low-income Black and Hispanic communities who have no ability to replace the pipes or escape the risk.
>
> They cope by spending **$500–$700/year on bottled water** — a workaround that doesn't protect them during bathing, cooking, or incidental ingestion, and which totals **$20+ billion per year** in consumer spending nationally. Or they use pitcher filters that most don't know are ineffective against lead unless specifically NSF/ANSI 53 certified.
>
> Meanwhile, the EPA's newest rule gives utilities **10 years** to complete lead service line replacement. There is no real-time, household-level, affordable monitoring system telling any of these 21 million people whether their water is safe on any given day. They are flying blind — paying for a service they cannot verify and a workaround that may not work.

## STEP 6: Workarounds and Why They Fall Short

| Current Workaround | Why It Fails |
|---|---|
| Annual Consumer Confidence Report (CCR) | Published months after collection; written to satisfy legal minimums, not to inform; uses averages that mask local variation; 8pt font in a mailed brochure nobody reads |
| Bottled water | Doesn't address bath/shower/cooking exposure; $500–700/year cost burden; environmental harm; still requires trusting a supply chain |
| Standard pitcher filter (Brita/PUR) | Most common filters (carbon only) do NOT remove lead unless NSF/ANSI 53 certified; consumer confusion is widespread and dangerous |
| Professional water test | $150–$300 per test, snapshot only, requires sampling skill, no access for renters, no ongoing monitoring |
| Boiling water | Only effective for microbiological contamination; does NOT remove lead, PFAS, nitrates, or heavy metals — and can concentrate them |
| Calling 311 / the city | Navigates bureaucracy, generates a case number, does not produce an answer in real time |
| Moving | Not available to low-income renters; and new residence may have same issue |

**The common thread:** Every existing solution requires the resident to either (a) spend money they don't have, (b) possess technical knowledge they don't have, (c) navigate a system designed for utilities — not households, or (d) accept permanent uncertainty and live with the anxiety.

**The gap in one sentence:** There is no affordable, real-time, household-accessible system that tells a renter in Chicago or Newark whether their tap water is safe *today* for their specific building and specific contaminants of concern.

## Primary Research Plan (This Weekend)

### Target Interview Profile
- 3–5 people who live in pre-1986 housing in an urban area
- Ideal: parents with children under 12, renters, OR school/childcare facility staff
- NOT: homeowners who recently replaced pipes, or people in rural areas with wells (different job)

### Questions to Ask (Open-Ended, No Solution Bias)

1. "Tell me about the last time you thought about your tap water. What was going on?"
2. "When did you start worrying about it? What changed?"
3. "Walk me through what you did about it."
4. "How do you feel when you turn on the tap right now?"
5. "What does 'fixed' look like to you?"

### What NOT to Ask
- "Would you use an app that monitors your water?" ← solution bias, invalidates the interview
- "Would you pay $X/month for clean water?" ← leads the witness
- "Do you think AI could help?" ← still locked away

### Where to Find Interviewees
- Facebook groups: "Flint Water Crisis Survivors," Newark NJ community groups, Chicago renters groups
- Reddit: r/chicago, r/newjersey, r/homeowners, r/Flint
- Local: parents at school dropoff, community center staff, tenant advocacy groups
- LinkedIn: School facility managers, public health nurses in urban districts

## Synthesis: The One-Sentence Problem Statement

> **21 million Americans in legacy municipal water systems — disproportionately low-income renters and families of color — currently have no reliable, affordable way to know whether their tap water is safe today; they cope by spending hundreds of dollars per year on bottled water and unverified filters that may not even address their specific contaminants, while waiting up to a decade for pipe replacements that may never reach their building.**
